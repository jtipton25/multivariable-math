The fundamental objects in this text are scalars, vectors, and matrices.

  1. Transpose Transivity: The transpose operator is transitive

\[ \begin{align*} (\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y})' & = \mathbf{x}' + \mathbf{y}' \end{align*} \]

x <- c(4, 3, 5)
y <- c(-4, 8, 7)
t(x + y)
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    0   11   12
t(x) + t(y)
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    0   11   12

In addition, the transpose of a transpose is the identity operator \(\left( \mathbf{x}' \right)' = \mathbf{x}\).

x
## [1] 4 3 5
t(x)
##      [,1] [,2] [,3]
## [1,]    4    3    5
t(t(x))
##      [,1]
## [1,]    4
## [2,]    3
## [3,]    5
  • Note: R can be finicky about vectors and matrices. The transpose converts the vector \(\mathbf{x}\) to a matrix \(\mathbf{x}'\) with one row. Thus, t(t(x)) gives a matrix with one column (which is a not quite the same as a vector). To force t(t(x)) to be a vector, you can drop the dimension with drop()
drop(t(t(x)))
## [1] 4 3 5
